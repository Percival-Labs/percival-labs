### Tweet 2

ATC works for aviation because:
- 45K flights/day (bounded)
- Physical constraints (runways, airspace)
- Human-speed decisions
- Government accountability

Now imagine 160M agents at machine speed. No physical constraints. No government oversight. ATC breaks before lunch.

---

### Tweet 3

Three problems with centralized agent coordination:

1. Who controls the controller? (Monopoly position → rent extraction)
2. Single point of failure (Coordinator goes down → everything stops)
3. Creates dependency, not capability (The guru model applied to infrastructure)

---

### Tweet 4

We already know how to coordinate billions of independent actors without a central controller.

It's called a market.

Price signals. Reputation. Contracts. Skin in the game. We solved this 500 years ago.

---

### Tweet 5

Map it to agents:

ATC: "You're cleared for runway 3"
Market: "This agent has $50K staked on its reliability"

ATC: Kill switch
Market: Stake gets slashed, trust destroyed

ATC: Compliance mandate
Market: Cooperators earn yield, defectors lose capital

### Tweet 6

The math:

Agent earns $1K/month, pays 5% activity fee to its backing pool.

$5K staked → 11.5% APY to stakers
$1K staked (early) → 60% APY

Early backers of good agents get paid. Bad agents get slashed.

No committee decides this. The economics decide.

---

### Tweet 7

Cold start problem? Solved by the same math.

Small pools = massive yields. Being first to back a good agent is extremely profitable.

The market rewards people who identify trust early — same mechanic that bootstrapped every successful marketplace.

---

### Tweet 8

Every orchestration framework (Swarms, LangGraph, CrewAI) coordinates agent *actions* — what they do.

A trust market coordinates agent *trust* — who they are, who backs them, what they're worth.

Actions are transient. Trust compounds.
