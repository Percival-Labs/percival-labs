## Long-Form Article (X Article or Blog)

### "You Already Have the Hard Part"

Everyone's scrambling to learn AI. Taking courses. Watching tutorials. Feeling behind.

But the people who should feel most confident right now — the ones with years of expertise in a specific field — are the ones sitting on the sidelines. They see developers on Twitter building agents and writing code, and they think *that's the thing I'm missing.*

It's not. And the longer you wait, the more it costs you.

---

**The scarcity flip**

For sixty years, the scarce resource in computing was the ability to write code. You needed a programmer for everything. That scarcity created an entire economy — CS degrees, coding bootcamps, six-figure developer salaries — all built around the fact that turning ideas into software required a specialized human.

That era is ending. Not slowly. Right now.

AI can produce code. And it's getting cheaper at a rate that should make every "learn to code" bootcamp nervous — token costs have dropped 50-200x in the last two years. The ability to produce software is no longer scarce. Anyone with a laptop and a Claude subscription can build an app.

So what IS scarce?

Knowing which app to build. Knowing which problems actually matter. Knowing the edge cases that only show up when you've done the work for years. Knowing what "good" looks like in a specific field.

That's domain expertise. And you can't buy it by the token.

---

**Three kinds of people in the AI economy**

As the dust settles, three roles are emerging.

**Orchestrators** design systems and manage AI agents. They spec outcomes, allocate budgets, evaluate quality. They're the founders and team leads of AI-native companies. They get a lot of attention.

**Systems Builders** create the infrastructure that orchestrators use — agent frameworks, evaluation pipelines, context management tools. They're engineers. They also get a lot of attention.

**Domain Translators** point AI at the right problems. They have deep expertise in a specific field — healthcare, construction, education, finance, agriculture, whatever — and they use that expertise to direct AI toward solutions that actually work.

A nurse who builds a patient triage workflow. A contractor who builds an estimating system. A teacher who builds a curriculum tool that adapts to how students actually learn.

They don't write code. They translate expertise into leverage.

Domain Translators get almost zero attention in the AI conversation. And they might be the most valuable of the three.

---

**You might already be one**

Think about what you know from your work. Not the textbook version — the real version. The shortcuts. The edge cases. The things that only show up after years of doing the job.

A nurse knows which intake questions actually predict what's wrong — not the ones on the form, the ones experience teaches you to ask. An electrician knows which code violations show up in which era of construction without checking a manual. A teacher knows in the first five minutes whether a lesson plan is going to hold a room.

That knowledge didn't come from a course. It came from years of doing the work, making mistakes, and developing judgment that no amount of reading can replicate.

That's exactly what AI can't do. It can generate code, draft emails, summarize documents, build apps. But it can't tell you which app to build. It doesn't know your patients, your job sites, your students, your market. It doesn't have your judgment.

You do. And that makes you more valuable in the AI economy than you think.

---

**Why the gap feels bigger than it is**

There's a reason domain experts feel locked out of AI. It's not an accident.

The AI education space runs on manufactured helplessness. Complexity is the business model. If it felt simple, nobody would pay $997 for a course. So the gurus add jargon, create frameworks, invent certification programs — anything to maintain the illusion that you need them.

"Prompt engineering" is the perfect example. It sounds technical. It sounds like a skill that requires training. But a good prompt is just clearly describing what you need. You do that every time you explain a problem to a coworker or walk a new hire through a process. If you can describe what good work looks like in your field, you can prompt AI.

The bridge between "domain expert" and "Domain Translator" is embarrassingly short. The people selling you maps for that bridge are making money off your hesitation, not your inability.

---

**What you actually need to learn**

If you have years of expertise in any field, here's what stands between you and using AI as a real tool — not a chatbot, a tool:

**Give it context.** Write down who you are, what you're working on, what you care about, what constraints you operate under. Plain English. In a text file. That's it. This is the single highest-leverage thing you can do, and it takes twenty minutes.

**Evaluate the output.** This is the part nobody talks about because it doesn't feel like a "skill." But knowing whether AI output is good, usable, and correct in your field? That IS your domain expertise. A nurse knows if a triage workflow makes clinical sense. A contractor knows if an estimate is realistic. A teacher knows if a lesson plan will actually hold a room. You already have this. It's the hard part.

**Build on what works.** Try something. If it's useful, do more of it. If it's not, try something different. This isn't engineering. It's the same iterative problem-solving you do in your work every day.

That's 80% of the value. No code. No courses. No certification.

---

**The real winners**

Here's what's going to happen over the next few years.

Code production costs will keep falling. AI fluency will become a baseline skill — like using a spreadsheet. Neither will be a competitive advantage.

The competitive advantage will be knowing where to aim the intelligence. Knowing the problems worth solving. Knowing what the AI doesn't know about your field. Having the judgment to catch when it's wrong and the expertise to make it right.

That's domain knowledge. The thing you already have. The thing you've been building for years while everyone else was telling you it wasn't enough.

If you're watching AI happen to other people because you think it "isn't for you" — you're sitting on the most valuable resource in the new economy and someone convinced you it was dirt.

It's not dirt. It's the hard part. And you already did it.

The AI fluency? That's the easy part. And it's getting easier every month.

Stop waiting for permission from tech Twitter. Your expertise is the scarce resource. Use it.
